# Lens: Cultural

How do people actually behave? What cultural factors shape decisions that economic models can't explain? This lens fills the gap between rational-actor theory and messy human reality.

> **Lineage:** This lens draws on behavioral economics (Kahneman, Tversky) — systematic deviations from rational choice; cultural dimensions theory (Hofstede, Inglehart) — measurable value differences across societies; and anthropological tradition (Geertz, Bourdieu) — thick description of how culture shapes meaning and action. It also borrows from social identity theory (Tajfel) and the sociology of institutions (DiMaggio, Powell). Key lesson from behavioral economics: people are predictably irrational, and the patterns of irrationality are often more informative than the rational model they violate.

## Independence Rule

Do not reference findings from previous lenses. Approach the question fresh. Your analysis should be able to stand alone.

## Core Questions
1. What cultural norms, values, or identities influence behavior on this topic?
2. How do different populations actually respond to this — not how models predict they should?
3. What behavioral biases are most relevant? (status quo bias, loss aversion, herd behavior, etc.)
4. What cultural context is being assumed in the mainstream framing of this issue?
5. Where does observed behavior deviate most from economic or rational expectations?
6. What social, identity, or tribal factors drive decisions that pure incentive analysis misses?

## How to Research Through This Lens
- Look for behavioral data: what people DO, not what they SAY they'll do (revealed vs stated preferences)
- Identify relevant cultural dimensions: individualism/collectivism, uncertainty avoidance, time orientation
- Check for behavioral biases that systematically distort responses to this issue
- Compare how different cultures frame and respond to the same phenomenon
- Look for social contagion effects: is this spreading through networks rather than rational adoption?
- Examine identity and tribal dynamics: is this issue a proxy for group identity?
- Find cases where policy or technology failed because it assumed rational actors (see [[source-evaluation]] — behavioral studies in Tier 2)

## Output Format
For each finding:
- BEHAVIOR: [what people actually do]
- DRIVER: [the cultural, psychological, or social force behind it]
- CULTURAL CONTEXT: [which populations, and why this context matters]
- DEVIATION FROM RATIONAL: [how and why this differs from economic/rational prediction]
- EVIDENCE: [behavioral data, surveys, ethnographic studies — with source and date]

Record behavioral data in [[data-points]] and cultural frameworks in [[concepts]].

## Voice
Observational. human. grounded in what people actually do, not what models say they should. "despite financial incentives to switch, 73% of users stayed with the default — consistent with status quo bias documented across 40 countries" not "people are irrational." describe the pattern, explain the mechanism.
