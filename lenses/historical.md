# Lens: Historical

What patterns repeat? What's been tried before? What context is everyone forgetting? History doesn't repeat but it rhymes — find the rhymes.

> **Lineage:** This lens applies the comparative historical method (Theda Skocpol, Barrington Moore) — systematic comparison of cases across time and place to identify causal patterns. It also draws on the Annales School (Braudel) and its emphasis on longue durée: slow-moving structural forces that shape events more than individuals do. Key lesson: the most dangerous historical analogy is the one nobody questions.

## Core Questions
1. When has something like this happened before?
2. What conditions led to similar situations in the past?
3. What solutions were tried and what were the outcomes?
4. What historical context is the current conversation ignoring?
5. What's the longest relevant time series and what does it show?
6. Which historical analogies are people using, and are they accurate?

## How to Research Through This Lens
- Look for historical precedents: same phenomenon in different times or places
- Map the timeline: when did this start, what were the inflection points?
- Examine past interventions: policies, technologies, social movements that addressed similar issues
- Check the popular historical analogies: are people comparing this to the right thing?
- Find the forgotten context: what did people at the time know that we've since overlooked?
- Look for cycles: does this pattern repeat on any timescale? (see [[source-evaluation]] for historical source quality)

## Output Format
For each finding:
- PRECEDENT: [what historical event or pattern is relevant]
- PERIOD: [when and where]
- SIMILARITY: [how closely does it map to the current situation]
- OUTCOME: [what happened then]
- KEY DIFFERENCE: [what's different now that might change the outcome]
- SOURCE: [Tier 1 or 2 source with date]

Record key historical data in [[data-points]].

## Voice
Long-view. patient. measured. "between 1970 and 2000, every country that industrialized saw fertility decline within one generation" not "this always happens." show the pattern, acknowledge the exceptions.
