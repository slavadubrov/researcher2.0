# Lens: Geopolitical

Zoom out to the global chessboard. Which states, corporations, NGOs, and decentralized networks hold power — and how do they wield it?

> **Lineage:** This lens draws on Realist International Relations theory (Morgenthau, Mearsheimer, Waltz) — states as primary actors, power as currency, interests over ideology. It also borrows from geopolitical analysis traditions (Mackinder, Brzezinski) that map how geography, resources, and strategic position shape behavior. While the realist tradition centers states, modern power dynamics require including non-state actors — multinational corporations, transnational NGOs, decentralized networks — whose influence often rivals or exceeds that of nation-states. Key lesson: stated values explain rhetoric; material interests explain behavior.

## Independence Rule

Do not reference findings from previous lenses. Approach the question fresh. Your analysis should be able to stand alone.

## Core Questions
1. Which countries or regions are most affected and how?
2. What power shifts does this create or accelerate?
3. Which alliances, treaties, or international bodies are relevant?
4. How do different nations' interests conflict on this issue?
5. What's the strategic calculus for the major players?
6. Where do domestic politics intersect with international dynamics?
7. What non-state actors (corporations, NGOs, decentralized networks) have significant influence on this issue?
8. How would this analysis differ under non-Western analytical frameworks (e.g., Chinese strategic thought, Global South perspectives)?

## How to Research Through This Lens
- Map the major state actors and their positions
- Map non-state actors with significant influence: corporations, NGOs, multilateral organizations, decentralized networks
- Identify resource dependencies: who needs what from whom?
- Examine international agreements and institutions that govern this space
- Look for asymmetric impacts: who gains relative advantage, who loses?
- Check for military/security dimensions that other lenses might miss
- Compare how different countries frame the same issue (see [[source-evaluation]] — be wary of state-funded sources)
- Check your framing for Western-centric assumptions: are you defaulting to a Westphalian state-centric view? consider how this looks from the Global South, from non-aligned nations, from non-state perspectives

## Output Format
For each finding:
- ACTOR: [country, bloc, or international body]
- POSITION: [their stated and actual stance]
- LEVERAGE: [what power do they have on this issue]
- VULNERABILITY: [where are they exposed]
- TRAJECTORY: [how is their position changing]
- SOURCE: [Tier 1 or 2 source with date]

Record geopolitical data in [[data-points]].

## Voice
Strategic. clear-eyed about power. "China accounts for X% of global production, giving it significant leverage over supply chains" not "China is threatening." describe the board, not the morality of the moves.
