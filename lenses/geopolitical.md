# Lens: Geopolitical

Zoom out to the global chessboard. Which countries, which power dynamics, which alliances and conflicts shape this topic?

> **Lineage:** This lens draws on Realist International Relations theory (Morgenthau, Mearsheimer, Waltz) — states as primary actors, power as currency, interests over ideology. It also borrows from geopolitical analysis traditions (Mackinder, Brzezinski) that map how geography, resources, and strategic position shape state behavior. Key lesson: stated values explain rhetoric; material interests explain behavior.

## Core Questions
1. Which countries or regions are most affected and how?
2. What power shifts does this create or accelerate?
3. Which alliances, treaties, or international bodies are relevant?
4. How do different nations' interests conflict on this issue?
5. What's the strategic calculus for the major players?
6. Where do domestic politics intersect with international dynamics?

## How to Research Through This Lens
- Map the major state actors and their positions
- Identify resource dependencies: who needs what from whom?
- Examine international agreements and institutions that govern this space
- Look for asymmetric impacts: who gains relative advantage, who loses?
- Check for military/security dimensions that other lenses might miss
- Compare how different countries frame the same issue (see [[source-evaluation]] — be wary of state-funded sources)

## Output Format
For each finding:
- ACTOR: [country, bloc, or international body]
- POSITION: [their stated and actual stance]
- LEVERAGE: [what power do they have on this issue]
- VULNERABILITY: [where are they exposed]
- TRAJECTORY: [how is their position changing]
- SOURCE: [Tier 1 or 2 source with date]

Record geopolitical data in [[data-points]].

## Voice
Strategic. clear-eyed about power. "China accounts for X% of global production, giving it significant leverage over supply chains" not "China is threatening." describe the board, not the morality of the moves.
