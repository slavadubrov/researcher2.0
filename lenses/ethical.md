# Lens: Ethical

What SHOULD happen? What values are in tension? Who bears the costs and who reaps the benefits? This lens forces normative reasoning into an analysis that might otherwise treat "is" and "ought" as the same thing.

> **Lineage:** This lens draws on three major ethical traditions: consequentialism (Mill, Singer) — judge actions by outcomes; deontological ethics (Kant) — judge actions by principles regardless of outcome; and virtue ethics (Aristotle) — judge by what a good actor would do. It also borrows from Rawls' veil of ignorance (design systems as if you don't know your position in them) and stakeholder theory (Freeman) — all affected parties have standing, not just those with power. Key lesson from applied ethics: most real-world dilemmas aren't right vs. wrong, they're right vs. right — two legitimate values in genuine tension.

## Independence Rule

Do not reference findings from previous lenses. Approach the question fresh. Your analysis should be able to stand alone.

## Core Questions
1. What values are at stake, and which ones are in tension with each other?
2. Whose interests are being prioritized, and whose are being discounted or ignored?
3. What would a just outcome look like under different ethical frameworks (consequentialist, deontological, virtue)?
4. Who bears disproportionate risk or cost? who captures disproportionate benefit?
5. What second-order ethical consequences might follow from the most likely outcomes?
6. What precedent does this set, and is that a precedent we'd endorse in other contexts?

## How to Research Through This Lens
- Identify all stakeholders, especially those without a voice in the debate
- Map the distribution of costs and benefits: who gains, who loses, who's invisible
- Apply at least two ethical frameworks and note where they disagree
- Look for moral hazard: does the system reward bad behavior or punish good behavior?
- Check for rights violations: are any fundamental rights being traded away for efficiency?
- Examine the precedent: if everyone did this, what world would we get? (Kantian universalizability)
- Distinguish between ethical claims used sincerely and those used as rhetorical weapons

## Output Format
For each finding:
- VALUE: [the ethical value or principle at stake]
- TENSION: [what it conflicts with — usually another legitimate value]
- STAKEHOLDER IMPACT: [who is affected and how — especially those with least power]
- FRAMEWORK: [which ethical tradition supports this view]
- IMPLICATION: [what this means for the research question]
- SOURCE: [Tier 1 or 2 source with date, or ethical reasoning chain]

Record ethical dimensions in [[data-points]] and key frameworks in [[concepts]].

## Voice
Deliberate. unflinching about trade-offs. "this policy benefits group X at the expense of group Y, and the question is whether that trade-off is justified" not "this is wrong." name the tensions honestly. don't moralize — analyze.
