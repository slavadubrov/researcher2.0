# Question Formulation

The quality of your research is bounded by the quality of your question. A vague question produces vague analysis regardless of how many lenses you apply. Sharpen the question BEFORE starting research.

> **Lineage:** This process adapts Philip Tetlock's superforecasting methodology — pre-committing to falsifiable predictions before seeing evidence — and the intelligence community's requirements definition process, which transforms vague policy questions into researchable intelligence requirements. Key lesson from Tetlock: forecasters who define their question precisely outperform those who "just start researching" by a wide margin.

## Step 1: Scope Narrowing

Your question is probably too broad. Narrow it.

- If it contains "and," it's probably two questions. split them
- If it could fill a textbook, add constraints: time period, geography, population, domain
- Replace "What about X?" with "What specific aspect of X, measured how, in what context?"
- Test: could two researchers interpret this question differently? if yes, it's too vague

### Narrowing Protocol
1. Write the question as-is
2. List 3 sub-questions it contains
3. Pick the ONE sub-question that matters most for your [[index|Output Goal]]
4. Rewrite as a specific, bounded question
5. Add explicit boundaries: what's IN scope and what's OUT

## Step 2: Assumption Surfacing

Before researching, write down what you already believe. This prevents confirmation bias from hiding in your blind spots.

For each assumption, ask:
- Where did I get this belief? (personal experience, news, expert, gut feeling)
- How confident am I? (high / medium / low)
- What tier source supports it? (see [[source-evaluation]])
- What would prove it wrong?

Document at least 3 assumptions. If you can't find any, you haven't looked hard enough — every question carries assumptions.

## Step 3: Success Criteria

Define what "done" looks like BEFORE you start:

- What would a GOOD answer include? (specific data? causal mechanism? actionable recommendation?)
- What would a BAD answer look like? (vague summary? single-source conclusion? no tensions?)
- What decision does this research inform? (see [[index|Output Goal]])
- Who is the audience and what do they need to act?

## Step 4: Pre-Commitment

Adapted from Tetlock's superforecasting: commit to what would change your mind BEFORE seeing evidence.

Write:
- "My current best guess is: [X]"
- "I would update toward [Y] if I found: [specific evidence]"
- "I would update toward [Z] if I found: [specific evidence]"
- "The strongest possible challenge to my current view would be: [what?]"

This creates a tripwire against confirmation bias. If during research you encounter your pre-committed evidence and DON'T update, you need to explain why.
