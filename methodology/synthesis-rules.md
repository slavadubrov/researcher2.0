# Synthesis Rules

How to combine findings across lenses without losing nuance. This is the hardest part of research — most people skip it and just stack facts.

> **Lineage:** This process adapts Richards Heuer's Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH) — systematically weighing evidence across multiple hypotheses rather than building a case for one. The Agreement/Tension Map structure borrows from ACH's evidence matrix. The confidence calibration follows Philip Tetlock's superforecasting methodology: explicit probability, stated assumptions, and pre-committed conditions for changing your mind. Key lesson from ACH: the hypothesis that best explains the evidence is not always the one with the most supporting evidence — it's the one with the least disconfirming evidence.

## The Synthesis Process

### Step 1: Lens Summary
After completing all 6 lenses, write a ONE paragraph summary per lens:
- What is this lens's main finding?
- Confidence level: high / medium / low
- What surprised you from this angle?

### Step 2: Agreement Map
Identify where lenses AGREE:
- If 5+ lenses point the same direction → high confidence finding
- If 3-4 lenses agree → moderate confidence, worth stating with caveats
- If only 1-2 lenses support a claim → hypothesis only, flag as uncertain

#### Agreement Resolution
When lenses agree, document WHY they agree. This matters because:
- **Independent evidence** = genuine convergence → high confidence
- **Shared assumptions** = potential groupthink → investigate the assumption
- **Same source** = single point of failure → find independent confirmation

Ask: "If I removed the shared assumption, would these lenses still agree?" If not, the agreement is weaker than it appears.

### Step 3: Tension Map
Identify where lenses DISAGREE:
- [[technical]] says X but [[economic]] says Y → this tension IS the insight
- Don't resolve by picking a winner. document both positions
- Ask: "under what conditions is each lens correct?"
- See [[contradiction-protocol]] for resolution framework

#### Tension Preservation
Preserve irreducible tensions as findings, not problems to solve. If after honest effort two lenses still disagree, that disagreement IS your finding. write it as: "This question has a genuine tension between [X] and [Y] that cannot be resolved with available evidence. The answer depends on [condition]."

A synthesis that resolves all tensions into neat agreement has probably destroyed its most valuable output.

#### Tension-Pair Reference Matrix

Common productive tensions across the 8 lenses:

| Lens A | Lens B | Typical Tension | What It Reveals |
|--------|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Technical | Economic | data says X but money flows toward Y | incentive misalignment |
| Technical | Cultural | model predicts X but people do Y | behavioral deviation |
| Economic | Ethical | profitable but harmful, or costly but right | values vs incentives |
| Economic | Geopolitical | markets want X but states want Y | policy vs reality gap |
| Historical | First Principles | always been this way vs fundamentals say shouldn't work | potential paradigm shift |
| Historical | Cultural | past pattern vs cultural change | whether history will rhyme |
| Geopolitical | Ethical | strategic interest vs moral obligation | realpolitik vs values |
| Geopolitical | Cultural | state-level analysis vs population-level behavior | top-down vs bottom-up |
| Contrarian | Technical | mainstream data interpretation vs alternative reading | measurement assumptions |
| Contrarian | Ethical | consensus moral framing vs who actually benefits | rhetorical vs genuine ethics |
| First Principles | Cultural | rational model vs actual behavior | theory vs practice |
| First Principles | Ethical | logical conclusion vs moral intuition | is vs ought |
| Ethical | Cultural | universal principles vs cultural norms | moral universalism vs relativism |
| Cultural | Economic | behavioral patterns vs incentive predictions | where homo economicus breaks |
| Contrarian | Historical | established pattern vs this-time-is-different | pattern persistence vs disruption |

Not all tensions appear in every project. Use this matrix to check: have you missed a productive tension?

### Step 4: Second-Order Insights
The best findings come from COMBINING lenses:
- "The technical data shows declining fertility, but the economic lens reveals that financial incentives haven't reversed it anywhere — which means the [[first-principles]] lens's argument about cultural shifts might be the dominant factor"
- These cross-lens insights are what make this system more powerful than reading 50 articles

### Step 5: Confidence Calibration
For each major finding, state:
- CLAIM: what you believe is true
- EVIDENCE: strongest supporting data (from [[data-points]])
- CONFIDENCE: high / medium / low
- WHAT WOULD CHANGE MY MIND: the specific evidence that would reverse this conclusion

### Step 6: Narrative Construction

Build the final narrative without flattening tensions:
- Lead with the strongest finding (highest confidence, most lens agreement)
- Present tensions as features, not problems: "The data points toward X, but economic incentives push toward Y — the resolution depends on [condition]"
- Your conclusion should read like a judge's opinion that acknowledges dissenting views, not a marketing document
- Separate clearly: what the evidence shows (objective) → what you interpret (subjective) → what remains unknown (honest)
- End with open questions, not false certainty

## Output Rules
- Never present a single-lens finding as a conclusion
- Always show the tension between lenses
- Separate "what the data shows" from "what I interpret"
- [[open-questions]] is as important as [[executive-summary]]
- Prefer "it seems likely that X because Y and Z, though A complicates this" over "X is true"

## Anti-Patterns (things that kill good research)
- Confirmation bias: only searching for evidence that supports your initial hunch
- Narrative fallacy: making a clean story out of messy, contradictory data
- Recency bias: overweighting the latest article over 30 years of data
- Authority bias: believing something because an impressive person said it
- Anchoring: letting the first number you found define your mental range
